# How Berkeley Built Its Homelessness Crisis

<p class="page-epigraph">Berkeley's solution to homelessness is a puzzle of such exquisite complexity that the only people who understand it are those whose salaries depend on it remaining unsolved.</p>

<!-- TODO: Several city document links in the endnotes are broken following a berkeleyca.gov site redesign. Verify and replace with Wayback Machine URLs or plain citations when bandwidth allows. -->

Berkeley's homelessness crisis did not arrive from outside. It was shaped, sustained, and in key respects manufactured by a sequence of local policy choices — weak enforcement, publicly funded adversarial litigation, a financial stack of ballot measures that rewards expansion and punishes accountability, and a political class whose personal interests aligned with the model at every stage. The neighborhood costs that resulted were not distributed evenly. They were concentrated on the corridors and residents least able to resist them, while the officials and voters who approved the system bore none of them.

---

For years, Berkeley was not merely reacting to homelessness pressures from the surrounding region; it was helping shape the incentives that concentrated those pressures inside the city. While neighboring jurisdictions often emphasized removal, Berkeley became known for a more permissive environment in which long-term vehicle dwelling, encampments, and repeated relocation cycles could persist with comparatively limited enforcement. This was especially visible in the corridor stretching from the Albany border through West Berkeley, where displacement from nearby cities intersected with Berkeley's political reluctance to impose consistent boundaries.<sup><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup><sup><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup>

## Vehicle Habitation and the Collapse of Enforcement

<div class="key-insight"><p>When a city stops enforcing a law and then repeals it, it has not merely tolerated a behavior — it has officially sanctioned it. Berkeley's formal repeal in 2022 converted years of implicit permission into explicit policy, and sent a legible signal to the region.</p></div>

A clear example was vehicle habitation. Berkeley had longstanding ordinances restricting residence in "house cars" and RV parking, yet these rules went largely unenforced for years. In 2022, the City Council formally repealed the old "house cars" ordinance and related restrictions, acknowledging that the framework had effectively collapsed. By then, the practical message had already been sent: Berkeley was one of the easier places in the East Bay to remain in a vehicle for extended periods.<sup><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup>

That mattered because policy signals shape migration patterns. Where enforcement is predictable, populations disperse; where enforcement is uncertain or politically constrained, populations concentrate.

The endpoint of that dynamic is documented by one of its participants. Amber Whitson, who moved to Berkeley from the Albany Bulb after Albany paid her $3,000 to relocate, and who subsequently became a plaintiff in litigation over Berkeley's RV enforcement practices, proposed in a February 2026 op-ed that Berkeley establish a large-vehicle shelter permit program capping overnight RV parking at whatever count a fresh census produces — no waitlist, no new entrants, phase-down toward zero as residents transition to housing.<sup><a href="#fn24">24</a></sup> The proposal's internal logic is sound: once a population has established itself without a defined ceiling over a decade, a cap is the only mechanism that permits managed reduction rather than perpetual displacement. The problem is timing. The correct cap was always zero — applied before the community formed, when enforcement was legally straightforward and politically manageable. Berkeley faces in 2026 the consequences of the choice it declined to make in 2012, and in nearly every year before or since.

A May 2025 piece argued for reopening a sanctioned encampment modeled on the SPARK safe parking lot at Grayson Street, which Whitson had lived in.<sup><a href="#fn25">25</a></sup> Her description of SPARK is notable for what it inadvertently reveals about policy design. The program worked, she argues, because participants were drawn from Berkeley's existing street population rather than through Alameda County's Coordinated Entry placement system, and because on-site staff screened out disruptive individuals. Both features — local-priority admission and behavior-based exclusion — are precisely what Berkeley's low-barrier, county-placement model explicitly rejects as discriminatory. What she identifies as SPARK's strength is what the city's own ideology has treated as a civil rights violation.

The common thread across both proposals is a claim to public space on the occupants' own terms: a permanent permit entitlement in one case, a city-funded self-selected community in the other. The underlying ask in each case is a version of what Albany cleared from the Bulb for $3,000 — reconstituted at substantially greater expense, and this time in Berkeley.

## The Private Burden of Public Health Costs

<div class="key-insight"><p>The public health costs of encampments fell on Berkeley's housed residents — closed parks, stench, disease risk, mandatory vaccinations, restricted outdoor access. None of these costs appeared in any city accounting of what the Berkeley Model costs. None were compensated. Most were never acknowledged.</p></div>

The environmental consequences of unmanaged vehicle habitation were documented as early as 2019, when an environmental scientist living in West Berkeley published an opinion piece noting that Aquatic Park had been closed to recreational use due to dangerous levels of E. coli and the intestinal pathogen enterococci — bacteria consistent with human fecal waste discharged into storm drains serving the park. The smell of raw sewage, he wrote, had become a navigational landmark: it arrived reliably when crossing Sacramento Street on the way home from downtown. City officials declined to formally connect the contamination to RV waste discharge, but acknowledged no plausible alternative source.<sup><a href="#fn26">26</a></sup>

Aquatic Park remained a recurring problem for the next seven years. In February 2026, Berkeley cleared the encampments around the park after three weeks of outreach by the Homeless Response Team. Eight people accepted short-term motel stays, billable to Alameda County. Several others left voluntarily. The city described the operation as "a great example of what the Homeless Response Team can do with the right resources and the right opportunity" — which raises the question of what prevented the same resources and opportunity from being deployed in 2019, 2020, 2021, 2022, 2023, 2024, or 2025.<sup><a href="#fn27">27</a></sup>

The eight motel placements are, separately, another data point on service uptake. A site that had been occupied long enough to close a public park and generate seven years of public health complaints produced eight people willing to accept indoor shelter. The others left for elsewhere in the city.

The Harrison Street corridor produced a different category of public health cost. In late 2025 and early 2026, Alameda County health officials detected a leptospirosis outbreak centered on the longstanding encampment near 8th and Harrison — a bacterial disease transmitted through rat urine, rare in developed countries, capable of causing kidney failure, liver failure, and meningitis in untreated cases. Two dogs within the encampment contracted the illness; one died. Rats carrying the pathogen were detected for the first time in five years in Alameda County. Berkeley's health department issued advisories recommending that residents within one-third of a mile of the epicenter treat standing water as contaminated, vaccinate pets, keep dogs on short leashes and carry their own water on walks, and disinfect any hard objects that had contacted ground-level mud with a bleach solution.<sup><a href="#fn28">28</a></sup>

Those costs — a leptospirosis vaccination series for a dog runs $25–$75 per annual booster, not counting the initial series or any diagnostic workup — were borne entirely by West Berkeley residents. They were not reimbursed. They did not appear in any city accounting of the Harrison encampment's costs. They were imposed on people who had no relationship to the encampment and no mechanism to recover them. The same is true of the vet bills incurred by anyone whose pet was exposed, the restricted recreational access at Aquatic Park across multiple summers, and the private decision of one resident to choose between opening his windows in summer heat or breathing in sewage.

The city's cost estimates for its homelessness programs have never included a line item for any of this. The same is true of the costs absorbed by public institutions. Following the Measure S defeat in November 2012, Berkeley's central public library — which had become a daytime destination for people with no place to store their possessions — adopted new rules of conduct limiting what patrons could bring inside. The library's deputy director described the behavior that prompted the change as "at least a daily occurrence, usually more": belongings left unattended throughout the building, blocking collections and computer terminals, making it impossible to clear the building at closing. The rules went into effect December 1, 2012 — weeks after the vote that preserved the status quo on the sidewalks outside.<sup><a href="#fn34">34</a></sup> Every patron who found a terminal occupied or a collection blocked absorbed a cost that appeared nowhere in any accounting of what Berkeley's enforcement posture produced.

In August 2025, the Berkeley City Council voted to transfer ownership of a 27,500-square-foot section of Carleton Street — a dead-end abutting the Bayer Pharmaceuticals campus — to Bayer, because the street had become home to a sizeable encampment the city could not or would not clear. A city staffer summarized the relocation plan at the council meeting: "We would have staff go out and talk with people. To the extent we had shelter opportunity, we would offer that, and to the extent we don't, we don't." That is a candid description of the city's position: if capacity exists, it will be offered; if not, the encampment continues and the adjacent landowner bears the cost. A CBS News report on the transfer quoted one encampment resident who described running an "outdoor thrift store" — selling and giving away items he described as found. The provenance of merchandise that accumulates "little by little over the course of seven or eight months via bike" on a dead-end street requires no elaboration. The city did not respond to press inquiries about timeline or process.<sup><a href="#fn38">38</a></sup>

The most direct and repeated imposition on a specific neighborhood was also the least visible in citywide accounting: Civic Center Park. The park sits directly across Martin Luther King Jr. Way from Berkeley High School and adjacent to City Hall. Across multiple encampment cycles beginning with Occupy Berkeley in 2011, the park was occupied, damaged, cleared, and restored — a cycle that each time required publicly funded police overtime, parks department hazmat cleanup, and grounds restoration. Those costs were never aggregated or reported. Each clearance was treated as a discrete event; the cumulative total was never presented.

The school proximity imposed costs that went beyond inconvenience. Civic Center Park is one of Berkeley High School's two designated emergency evacuation sites. With 1,600 students on an open campus, the school depends on access to the park in an emergency. During encampment periods, that access was functionally compromised. The school's principal communicated concerns to the city; the encampments continued. Parents noted that the evacuation site could not absorb hundreds of students while occupied by tents and a population that law enforcement described, in one officer's account, as a "who's who of the criminal homeless."<sup><a href="#fn29">29</a></sup> The city's response was that it intended to be thoughtful and collaborative.<sup><a href="#fn30">30</a></sup>

Directly across the street from the park stands a men's overnight shelter. On the block bounded by the park, the shelter, and City Hall, the encampment cycles left behind damage that extended to the public buildings themselves — including physical desecration of City Hall's exterior by encampment-affiliated activists during clearance operations. The tot playground adjacent to this corridor exists on city maps. Whether it functions as a place parents bring children is a different question, one the city has not answered and has not asked.

## Regional Inflow, Presented as a Local Problem

<div class="key-insight"><p>Berkeley's encampment population was regional from the start. Framing it as a local housing crisis defined the response — and precluded the cost-sharing, residency requirements, and regional coordination that would have followed from an accurate diagnosis.</p></div>

Berkeley was slow to publicly acknowledge how regional many of its encampments had become. Residents and advocates were often presented in civic debate as if they were primarily longstanding Berkeley community members, even though reporting and litigation records showed continuity with earlier encampments in Albany and elsewhere. Amber Whitson, for example, was documented as having lived for years at the Albany Bulb before later becoming a prominent figure in Berkeley's marina and RV community politics.<sup><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> The broader point is not any one individual, but that Berkeley frequently discussed a regional inflow as though it were an entirely local housing phenomenon.

The framing obscured a population that is not merely regional but deliberately mobile. Sociologists and service providers refer to it as the "West Coast Circuit" — a seasonal pattern of travel between nodes in Portland, the Bay Area, Los Angeles, and San Diego. Within this subculture, participants call themselves "travelers" or, if vehicle-dwelling, "rubber tramps." For a traveler moving along the West Coast, Berkeley was a known high-value node: mild climate, minimal enforcement, high-service density, and a political culture that did not distinguish between a person who had lived in Berkeley for a decade and one who had arrived last week. The economic logic of circuit riding is specific — Berkeley was not a destination for permanent housing but a service depot where a traveler could recover resources, access medical care, and wait out conditions elsewhere without friction.

The non-congregate premium Berkeley eventually committed to gave the circuit a specific new incentive. A traveler choosing between a floor mat in a congregate shelter in another city and holding out for a private motel room in Berkeley faced a straightforward calculation — and Berkeley's political trajectory made the wait rational. The city's insistence that only private rooms were humane did not merely reflect a quality preference; it created a competitive advantage for Berkeley relative to every other node on the circuit.

The Measure S debate in 2012 surfaced this dynamic explicitly, then buried it. Proponents of the sit/lie ordinance argued that the most disruptive street behavior was disproportionately attributable to travelers rather than locally-rooted residents. The "No on S" coalition, led by Arreguín, countered that targeting transients would inevitably harm long-term Berkeley residents who had nowhere else to go. Both claims had merit. Neither side addressed the structural implication: a policy environment calibrated to protect the most locally-rooted residents would, without residency requirements, provide identical protection to anyone who arrived that morning. The "civil rights" framing that defeated Measure S did not distinguish between the two populations, and the service infrastructure built on its aftermath was designed on the same non-distinction.

The circuit itself is not new in Berkeley. Telegraph Avenue's "gutter punk" community of the 1990s and early 2000s — young, mobile, deliberately countercultural — represented an earlier iteration of the same subculture. That population was eventually displaced from the avenue by a combination of enforcement and changing street economics. The vehicle-dwelling traveler who came to occupy the West Berkeley corridor is the same phenomenon with different equipment. Berkeley's decade-long tolerance for long-term vehicle habitation — culminating in the 2022 repeal of its house car ordinance — converted its arterial streets into the physical infrastructure that circuit riding requires.

Overlapping with the circuit rider population, but distinct from it, is a group that is not homeless in any meaningful sense: the voluntary vehicle dweller, or "boondocker" — someone who chooses to live in a van or RV as a lifestyle rather than a necessity, often while earning income from remote work. In rural settings, boondocking typically means camping on Bureau of Land Management land without hookups. Berkeley offered an urban equivalent: 5G coverage, proximity to amenities, a world-class waterfront, and, crucially, no enforcement. The Berkeley Marina and the frontage roads along I-80 were documented in vehicle-dwelling community platforms — most notably the iOverlander app and a substantial body of VanLife YouTube content — as an urban destination specifically recommended for its "chill vibes" and absence of police attention relative to San Francisco and other Bay Area cities. That reputation was self-propagating: positive reviews attracted more travelers, which deepened the community's presence, which generated more positive reviews.

The policy implication was invisible to the city because the city made no attempt to see it. Berkeley's service infrastructure drew no distinction between the person who had been sleeping on the street for five years with nowhere to go and the remote worker who had parked a converted Sprinter van at the marina to avoid paying rent while drawing a tech salary. The ordinance repeal that advocates framed as protecting the destitute applied with equal force to the voluntarily off-grid. The count of "vehicle-dwelling residents" that justified continued non-enforcement and service expansion included both. The city's refusal to distinguish between populations with fundamentally different needs — one requiring intervention, the other requiring nothing more than a parking ticket — was not a neutral act. It was a policy choice that transferred public value to people who had not asked for help, while using their presence to justify spending on people who had.

## The Magnet Effect and Regional Migration

<div class="key-insight"><p>Premium services without residency requirements are not just a local policy — they are a regional recruitment signal. Berkeley's service density made relocation from neighboring cities a rational individual decision, one the city chose not to measure, contain, or price into its cost estimates.</p></div>

The migration dynamic was not merely inferred — it was documented in Berkeley's own press. A 2017 Berkeleyside profile followed James Moore, who moved his family from Union City to Berkeley deliberately, citing the city's service density: "showers, food, and clothes all in one area." He described Berkeley as the "Promised Land" — a place where the combination of accessible services, a non-punitive environment, and the promise of eventual permanent housing justified the move from a neighboring city that offered none of the same.<sup><a href="#fn15">15</a></sup>

The structural implication is straightforward: when a city offers materially better services than its neighbors, without residency requirements, it assumes a share of the regional population that would otherwise be distributed across multiple jurisdictions. Each person who relocates to access Berkeley services becomes a case that costs the city's general fund — in shelter beds, police response, fire response, EMS, and social services — while the originating jurisdiction incurs none of those costs.

Berkeley's city government was aware of this dynamic and chose not to address it with residency requirements or regional cost-sharing mechanisms. The Homeless Services Panel of Experts, whose mandate focuses on service quality rather than fiscal sustainability, had no institutional interest in raising the question. The result was a system that delivered on its promise to individuals like Moore — and transferred the cost of that promise entirely to Berkeley residents and taxpayers.

## Publicly Funded Adversarial Litigation

<div class="key-insight"><p>Berkeley funded legal organizations that successfully constrained the city's own enforcement options. Each constraint became a justification for more spending. The city did not merely fail to resist this dynamic — it financed it.</p></div>

Another unusual feature of Berkeley's approach was the overlap between public funding and adversarial litigation. Berkeley and related city bodies funded legal-service organizations and firms for tenant and homelessness-related work, while some of the same advocacy networks or aligned counsel were also involved in lawsuits challenging Berkeley's own encampment enforcement. That arrangement may be legally permissible, but politically it created a circular system in which the city financed institutions that could simultaneously oppose city policy in court.<sup><a href="#fn4">4</a></sup>

## The Financial Stack

<div class="key-insight"><p>Six overlapping ballot measures cover every phase of the pipeline — planning, construction, operations, expansion. When one stream is exhausted or a program fails, another absorbs it without a public audit. The architecture makes system-level accountability structurally impossible.</p></div>

Berkeley's homelessness strategy is not funded by a single measure — it is supported by a layered set of voter-approved taxes and bonds, each covering a different phase of the pipeline. Together they constitute a self-reinforcing fiscal architecture that has proved durable across election cycles and administrations.

| Measure | Year | Type | Primary role |
|---------|------|------|-------------|
| **U1** | 2016 | Business tax on large landlords | Funds the Housing Trust Fund; pays for pre-development and design of projects years before groundbreaking |
| **T1** | 2016/2020 | Infrastructure bond | Site preparation; funded electrical and facility upgrades enabling rapid deployment of modular shelter units like the STAIR Center |
| **P** | 2020 | Real estate transfer tax | Operations: the $110/day motel leases, service contracts, the SCU |
| **O** | 2018 | $135M general obligation bond | Construction: gap financing for nonprofit developers building permanent supportive housing, including the Hope Center on Berkeley Way |
| **L** | 2022 | $650M bond ("Vision 2050") | Expansion: ~$200M allocated to affordable and social housing; intended to move people out of interim motels into permanent units |
| **W** | 2022 | Vacant homes tax | Secondary revenue stream to the Housing Trust Fund; administered by the Rent Board |

The logic of the stack is that each phase has its own dedicated funding: U1 pays for planning, O and L pay for building, P and W pay for operating. When one stream is exhausted or politically constrained, the others remain. This architecture has a structural consequence: **program-level failure cannot propagate to system-level accountability.**  When the SCU collapsed under budget pressure, its functions were absorbed by county systems without triggering a public audit of what the city had spent. When state Encampment Resolution Funding for the Super 8 lease expires, Measure P covers the gap. There is always another measure.

The Hope Center on Berkeley Way — listed in the Measure O column above — illustrates how that logic was embedded in the process from the beginning. The council first considered the project in September 2013, as an "innovative housing and services center" to be built on a 112-spot downtown parking lot. No funding had been identified. Mayor Bates asked the direct question: "Why would you start doing something if you could never afford to do it?" Councilmember Maio's answer stated the governing principle plainly: "You never start out knowing where the money's going to come from… I think that's what this does. We're getting started."<sup><a href="#fn33">33</a></sup> The initial cost estimate was approximately $50 million. The project would eventually be funded by Measure O — the $135 million bond Berkeley voters approved in 2018, five years after the council voted to get started without a plan. The Hope Center opened in September 2022, nearly a decade after that vote. Final cost: approximately $120 million total, with the Hope Center component specifically accounting for $71 million of that. The 53 units of permanent supportive housing within the Hope Center came in at $738,044 per unit. The project serves approximately 97 clients at capacity.

The operational economics are instructive in ways Berkeley's public presentations have not made explicit. Placing the Hope Center alongside the two converted motel sites included in this report's police data analysis reveals the inversion that drives the system's cost structure:

| Property | Units | Capital/Unit | Annual Operating/Unit | 10-Year Total | 20-Year Total |
|----------|-------|-------------|----------------------|---------------|---------------|
| Golden Bear Inn (San Pablo) | 48 | ~$413,000 | ~$37,500 | ~$788,000 | ~$1,163,000 |
| Rodeway Inn (University Ave) | 43 | ~$488,000 | ~$45,260 | ~$941,000 | ~$1,393,000 |
| Hope Center PSH | 53 | ~$738,044 | ~$27,500 | ~$1,013,000 | ~$1,288,000 |

The motel conversions required less capital but cost more to operate annually. Over a 20-year horizon Hope Center PSH is cheaper than the Rodeway Inn — the conversion with the lowest upfront cost becomes the most expensive long-term option. The city that justified motel conversions as cost-effective alternatives to new construction was correct in the short term and wrong over any planning horizon that extends to permanence. None of these facilities is temporary.

The emergency shelter beds within the Hope Center cost $45,000–$55,000 per bed annually to operate — more than the motel leases. Berkeley's defenders of both models invoke a "hospital diversion" efficiency argument: a chronically homeless individual costs an estimated $60,000–$100,000 per year in emergency room visits, police interventions, and county jail stays; at $27,500 per year, a PSH bed is, by that logic, a net fiscal save. The argument may be valid for the specific population the Hope Center serves. It has not been applied to evaluate whether the converted motel occupants represent that same high-utilization population, or whether housing them produces comparable diversion. The efficiency argument is claimed for the program with the highest capital cost; it has not been used to audit any of the others.

Over a ten-year period, the total public investment per Hope Center PSH resident is approximately $1.04 million — $738,000 in capital plus roughly $300,000 in operating costs. The city that could not afford to fix its city hall, that watched its public library restrict access because it had no other mechanism to manage encampment displacement, and that now rents council chambers from the school board, has committed over a million dollars per person served in its flagship homeless housing program. The project that began as an unfunded aspiration became a ballot measure that became a capital commitment, with the financial stack assembled in the years between. The pattern is not an aberration; it is the system operating as designed.


Each of these measures also carries its own oversight committee or panel of experts, all drawing from the same small pool of politically connected appointees. Cross-panel membership ensures that the funding priorities of one measure reinforce those of another. An appointee who helped design a project under Measure O has an interest in ensuring Measure P funds its operations. The oversight structure that is nominally independent is, in practice, a coordinated constituency.

Every one of these measures passed by wide margins. That durability is partly explained by Berkeley's political culture — but it is also a structural feature of how the opposition is constituted. The formal opposition argument in Berkeley ballot pamphlets for each of these measures was written by the same signatory: Marcus Crawley of the Alameda County Taxpayers' Association, operating in the tradition of the Howard Jarvis Taxpayers' Association and Proposition 13. That lineage is politically toxic to Berkeley voters, who associate it with the legacy of anti-government fiscal conservatism. As a result, a "No" vote on any of these measures reads, in Berkeley's political context, as a vote against housing the homeless or maintaining streets — regardless of what the opposition argument actually says.

The consequence is that the substantive accountability critique — the absence of failure criteria, the cross-measure bailout structure, the lack of outcome metrics — has never been effectively made to Berkeley voters as a standalone argument. It arrives pre-bundled with ideology most Berkeley residents reject, and loses. The financial stack has expanded measure by measure not because voters evaluated each program's performance, but because the opposition was never credibly separated from its anti-tax framing.

The ballot pamphlet process itself forecloses alternatives. Under California election law, the opposition argument is filed on a first-come basis; whoever submits first controls the slot. At least one Berkeley resident directly affected by the policies at issue attempted to file the opposition argument for one of these measures and found Crawley had already claimed it — and was unwilling to yield to someone who would actually live with the consequences. The argument that reached voters was Crawley's generic anti-tax framing, not the neighborhood impact case the affected resident would have made. The structural accountability critique that could have persuaded Berkeley voters was never submitted, because the person best positioned to make it had no guaranteed path to the page.

The proponent side reinforces this dynamic through deliberate rhetorical bundling. Berkeley's housing ballot measures consistently invoke teachers, firefighters, and other public servants — workers whose housing difficulty is real and relatable — to build support for bonds whose funds flow predominantly to the most expensive tier of the housing spectrum: permanent supportive housing at $738,000 per door at the Hope Center — the city's own flagship project — and operating subsidies for high-intensity shelter at $110 per room per night. The sympathetic image and the fiscal reality are not the same thing, and the measures are not written to make the distinction clear.

| Ballot language | The implied beneficiary | Where the money goes |
|----------------|------------------------|----------------------|
| "Affordable housing" | Local teacher in a 1-bedroom apartment | Below-market-rate units requiring $70,000+ income to qualify |
| "Workforce housing" | City employees priced out of Berkeley | Often the smallest share of units actually built |
| "Social housing" | Mixed-income buildings for community members | High-density projects with deep inclusionary requirements that frequently stall |
| "Supportive housing" | Rarely featured in campaign materials | $110/day motel rooms with 24/7 clinical staffing |

The firefighter framing carries its own irony. Berkeley firefighters earn median base salaries of $110,000–$122,000, with total compensation including overtime and benefits frequently exceeding $180,000.<sup><a href="#fn21">21</a></sup> Research on Bay Area fire departments suggests a substantial majority of firefighters live outside the cities they serve — often by choice, in less dense suburban or rural areas — rather than because they are priced out of a studio apartment in a transit corridor.<sup><a href="#fn22">22</a></sup> Using this demographic as the face of housing scarcity is effective precisely because it is difficult to argue against. It is also imprecise about what the measures actually fund.

## The Opportunity Cost of the Stack

<div class="key-insight"><p>Every ballot measure that directs funding to the homelessness pipeline is also a decision not to fund something else. Berkeley's structural deficit and deteriorating infrastructure exist alongside a financial stack that has expanded continuously for a decade. The city has never presented voters with that tradeoff explicitly.</p></div>

Each of these measures was presented to voters as additive — new revenue for a specific purpose, not a reallocation of existing capacity. In practice, every dollar committed to the homelessness pipeline through Measure P, U1, O, L, or W is a dollar that cannot be spent on streets, sidewalks, drainage, parks, libraries, or deferred maintenance. Berkeley faces a structural deficit and a backlog of deteriorating infrastructure. The financial stack did not cause that deficit in isolation, but it consumed fiscal capacity that might otherwise have addressed it. Critics argued throughout this period that Berkeley was systematically choosing downstream crisis management over upstream civic maintenance — and that the visible deterioration of the city's physical fabric reflected that choice.<sup><a href="#fn5">5</a></sup>

The police cost is not merely a future fiscal abstraction — it is already visible in the budget the city says it cannot afford. As of April 2026, Berkeley is navigating a $29 million structural deficit. Among the proposals on the table: permanently eliminate 32 vacant police positions and 20 firefighter positions, cementing the department's current understaffing as the new baseline. Berkeley PD is already operating at roughly 144 sworn officers against an authorized complement of 174, with only about 54 assigned to patrol and 7–8 officers covering the entire city on any given shift. A Priority 1 call — robbery, assault, mental health crisis — typically draws 3–4 officers for an hour or more, consuming roughly half the city's available patrol capacity. District 4, which contains the University Avenue shelter cluster, generates the highest call volume in the city; when that district pulls officers to manage an incident, response times degrade citywide. The city is simultaneously funding the facilities that generate those calls, declining to fill the positions needed to answer them, and describing the deficit as a structural problem beyond any single budget line. The connection between those three facts has not been presented to Berkeley voters or taxpayers in any public document.

No example makes the opportunity cost more concrete than Berkeley's own city hall. The 1909 Willis Polk building at 2134 Martin Luther King Jr. Way — a Baroque Revival landmark and one of the most architecturally significant civic structures in the East Bay — was allowed to deteriorate without the seismic upgrades it required. By the time of Arreguín's tenure, structural engineers had determined it would require at least $100 million to bring it to modern seismic standards; without that work it was at risk of collapse — a "pancake" failure — in a major earthquake. The building has been vacant since. Berkeley City Council now holds its meetings in rooms rented from the Berkeley Unified School District.

The Premier Cru purchase at 1011 University — the $3.1 million acquisition that became a nonprofit services hub — was explicitly described at the time as a step toward moving city functions out of Old City Hall, whose seismic deficiencies were already known. That plan failed. City Hall remained vacant. In his final year as mayor, Arreguín proposed using the seismically condemned building as a warming shelter for homeless residents — the structure judged too dangerous to host elected officials offered as housing for Berkeley's most medically vulnerable population. The proposal was not adopted. A plan to renovate the civic center area is now under discussion, requiring a bond measure — adding to the debt load that the motel leases, the ARPA expenditures, and the existing ballot measure stack have already committed future Berkeley taxpayers to. One generation's deferred maintenance becomes the next generation's bond obligation, and the programs built during the period of neglect continue to run regardless.

## Land-Use Choices and Revenue Capacity

<div class="key-insight"><p>Converting a commercial property to nonprofit shelter is a permanent revenue decision, not a temporary one. The property leaves the tax rolls; the Transient Occupancy Tax disappears; future Measure P transfer tax on a high-value redevelopment sale is never generated. Those losses were never presented alongside the facility costs.</p></div>

Land-use choices reflected the same preference structure. Rather than prioritizing redevelopment of certain underperforming sites for higher tax yield, employment activity, or broader economic return, Berkeley at times converted motels and similar properties into shelters or interim housing. The former Super 8 on University Avenue is a clear example: a commercial lodging property became part of the city's shelter system. Supporters viewed that as pragmatic humanitarian use; critics viewed it as another instance of reducing future revenue-generating capacity in favor of costly short-term management.<sup><a href="#fn6">6</a></sup>

For contrast: east of Martin Luther King Jr. Way on University Avenue, a former strip mall was redeveloped into the Trader Joe's anchored complex. That project generated measurable improvement in foot traffic and business activity in the surrounding blocks. West of MLK, the city made a different choice.

What made the motel owners willing to make that deal is itself part of the story. Lodging on University Avenue west of MLK was already struggling before the conversions began — but the conversions accelerated the deterioration of the remaining properties' commercial viability. Transient lodging is acutely sensitive to corridor character. A traveler booking on Expedia compares neighboring options; increased police activity, changed street demographics, and visible distress in adjacent blocks move bookings away from a corridor regardless of what any individual property does. Each conversion generated the externalities that made the next property's owners more receptive to a city lease offer. The city was not merely identifying willing sellers among already-distressed motel operators — it was, through its own earlier decisions, manufacturing the conditions that produced willing sellers. The process was self-reinforcing in the way Justin Herman's urban renewal designations were self-reinforcing: once authorities signal that a corridor is being reallocated, private investment stops, deferred maintenance accumulates, and the properties become progressively easier to acquire on public terms. The "blight" that justified the intervention was partly an artifact of the intervention itself.

The cumulative result of those choices is a corridor identity that no one ever voted on. Within a half-mile stretch of University Avenue west of MLK, Berkeley has placed interim housing at 1461, reentry housing at 1619, interim housing at 1761, and a subsidized nonprofit services hub at 1011 — plus the former Rodeway Inn conversion nearby. The STAIR navigation center operated on adjacent Second Street. These decisions were each approved individually, with individual justifications, on individual council agendas. At no point were Berkeley voters presented with the aggregate as a concept and asked to ratify it: *this corridor will become the city's primary concentration of homeless services.*

North Berkeley has the Gourmet Ghetto — a district identity that emerged from deliberate choices about what kinds of businesses and uses would define that part of the city, and that is celebrated as an asset. University Avenue's identity as a homeless services corridor is also the product of deliberate choices, made one facility at a time, by a council that never named the pattern it was creating. The difference is that the Gourmet Ghetto was presented to the neighborhood as a vision. The services corridor was not presented to anyone. It accumulated.

Whether that accumulation reflects a coherent strategy — cluster services to share infrastructure and outreach capacity — or simply the path of least political resistance, concentrating facilities in a district whose residents lacked the political weight to refuse them, has never been publicly explained. The question has not been asked in any council proceeding. The corridor's identity, as the primary approach to one of the world's great public universities, was treated as irrelevant to the siting calculus.

## Institutionalized Advocacy: The Measure P Expert Panel

<div class="key-insight"><p>The Homeless Services Panel of Experts is drawn from the same advocacy community that opposes policing as a policy tool. Asking it to weigh policing costs against program benefits is asking people with a structural interest in one answer to consider the other. Data that does not flatter the model is not sought — and when it surfaces, it has no institutional home.</p></div>

Measure P, approved by Berkeley voters in 2020, does something unusual even by Berkeley's standards: it creates a permanent "homeless services panel of experts" with a designated advisory role over how the tax proceeds are spent. No comparable city has written an unelected advocacy body with a direct financial stake in continued spending into the governing structure of a tax measure.

The practical effect is to institutionalize a constituency for homelessness spending within the fiscal mechanism that funds it. Panel members have every incentive to recommend expanding services and approving new facilities; they have no institutional mandate to weigh neighborhood impact, fiscal sustainability, or opportunity cost. Elected officials retain nominal authority, but the political cost of overriding a panel of credentialed experts is real.

This is distinct from ordinary lobbying. It is a governance structure that builds the homeless services sector into the allocation process as a participant — not merely an interested party.

The data problem runs deeper than incomplete reporting. When the City Council and the Panel evaluate spending decisions, the costs they consider are fiscal — draws on the general fund, restricted funds, or grant allocations. Incremental policing loads, EMS demand, fire response, and neighborhood public safety impact are not line items in these deliberations. They are externalities that fall on a different budget and a different constituency. The panel's membership is drawn heavily from the same progressive advocacy community that has actively worked to reduce police department capacity and scope. The result is not merely a gap in the analysis — it is an active institutional disinterest in the data that would make the full cost visible.

The City Council is subject to the same dynamic. Councilmember Rashi Kesarwani campaigned explicitly on a commitment to "data-based decision-making." When the first renewal vote for the STAIR/Pathways program came before the council, no outcome data had yet been produced by the operator. Kesarwani voted to renew, explaining that the funding decision had to be made at that moment — before the data the program had been designed to generate was available. The pilot's evaluation window and its funding renewal deadline were structurally misaligned, ensuring the council could not make a data-informed decision even if it had intended to.

## How Non-Congregate Became Legally Entrenched

<div class="key-insight"><p>By funding organizations that litigated for non-congregate shelter as an ADA right, Berkeley helped convert a policy preference into a legal mandate — and locked itself permanently into a price point it could have avoided.</p></div>

The shift from traditional congregate shelter to private-room models was not purely a policy preference — it was partly engineered through litigation. The Grayson Street Shelter, sponsored by then-Councilmember Kate Harrison, was originally conceived as a congregate facility. COVID-19 provided the initial justification for de-densification: public health protocols required separating shelter residents into individual spaces. That emergency accommodation hardened into a client expectation and, more consequentially, into a litigation posture.

Attorneys representing unhoused individuals began invoking the Americans with Disabilities Act to argue that clients with histories of incarceration or institutional trauma suffered from documented psychological disabilities — PTSD, anxiety disorders — that made shared sleeping spaces medically contraindicated. Under this framing, a private room with a locking door was not a preference but a "reasonable accommodation" required by federal law. Cities that offered only congregate beds could be sued for ADA non-compliance. Berkeley, having already funded the legal organizations advancing this argument, was structurally positioned to capitulate to it.

The practical result was that congregate shelter — which costs a fraction of $110 per room per night — became legally vulnerable as a primary offering. The non-congregate mandate that locks Berkeley into its current price point was not simply imposed by activist pressure; it was constructed through litigation made possible in part by city-funded counsel, then used to justify the motel leases that Measure P now funds.

## The Geography of Sacrifice: Who Bears the Costs

<div class="key-insight"><p>The Berkeley Model persists because it is rational for every participant in the coalition: the Hills voter gets to feel compassionate at no neighborhood cost; the Hills politician earns progressive credentials with no constituents bearing consequences; the service provider gets contracts; the expert panel gets relevance and a campaignable résumé line. The only parties for whom the calculus is negative are the residents of the corridors where the facilities land.</p></div>

Berkeley's homelessness infrastructure is not distributed evenly across the city. The converted motels, navigation centers, and interim housing facilities are concentrated in the industrial and transit corridors of West and South Berkeley — District 4, primarily — while the residential neighborhoods of the Berkeley Hills and the flatlands east of Telegraph remain largely unaffected.

This is not accidental. The non-congregate mandate's high per-unit cost functions as a de facto geographic filter. At $110 per room per night, only commercial properties with existing room stock — motels — are financially viable conversion targets. Motels are not located in wealthy residential neighborhoods. They are located on arterial corridors in working-class and mixed commercial areas. The policy model that champions non-congregate shelter as the only humane option simultaneously ensures that the facilities will land in the neighborhoods least positioned to resist them politically.

The Oxford Elementary School site illustrates the dynamic in reverse. After the school was closed due to earthquake risk, the 2-acre property became a candidate for public repurposing. Under the Naylor Act, the school district was required to offer the site to other public agencies before selling it privately — giving the City of Berkeley a right of first refusal for potential use as a shelter or navigation center. The councilmember for District 5, whose affluent residential neighborhood surrounds the site, allowed that right of refusal to lapse without acting on it. The property was subsequently sold for market-rate housing development.

No shelter will be built on Oxford Street. The expensive, high-intensity shelter experiments and the public safety friction they generate remain in West Berkeley. The officials who champion these policies at the citywide level have been notably effective at ensuring their own districts are not asked to host them.

This geographic separation produces a durable political dynamic. Residents of Berkeley's hillside and upper-flatlands neighborhoods vote for these measures and the politicians who sponsor them, but bear none of the neighborhood costs — no increase in calls for service, no shift in street character, no change in the adjacent businesses they frequent. The compassion is real; the cost is someone else's. Politicians representing those neighborhoods can support shelter expansion, collect credibility with progressive colleagues, and accumulate political capital for future negotiations — all at zero cost to their constituents. The cost-benefit calculation for a District 5 or District 6 councilmember supporting a District 4 shelter siting is entirely different from what it would be if the facility were in their own ward. The coalition that sustains the Berkeley Model is not irrational — it is rational for everyone in it except the residents who actually live near the sites.


The district map adds a further layer of diffusion. University Avenue runs along the boundary between Districts 1 and 4; the shelter cluster sits in the seam between them. District 3 contains the Grayson Street facility. No single councilmember has unambiguous ownership of the corridor. Each can defer to the adjacent representative, refer decisions to citywide bodies, or treat the University Avenue impacts as a shared problem that is therefore no one's primary responsibility. Boundary siting is not a conspiracy — it is a foreseeable consequence of placing facilities on arterial corridors that double as district lines — but the accountability gap it creates is real and has not been addressed.

## A Selective Transparency

<div class="key-insight"><p>Berkeley's spending decisions are well-documented in the public record. What is absent from that record is any accounting of the policy choices that made the spending necessary — the enforcement failures, the litigation funding, the regional placement dynamics. Transparency about solutions is not the same as accountability for causes.</p></div>

Berkeley's city government is, in one sense, unusually forthcoming. Lease terms, funding sources, grant mechanisms, and budget allocations are documented in council packets and publicly available. The $976,580 first-year lease for the Super 8 conversion, the Measure P fallback for years 3–5, the damage deposit schedule — all of this is in the public record.

What is not documented, discussed in council chambers, or presented to voters is the other side of the ledger: the policy choices that created the conditions requiring the response. The lax enforcement of vehicle habitation ordinances. The funding of organizations that litigated against the city's own encampment policies. The regional placement dynamics that brought non-Berkeley residents into Berkeley facilities at Berkeley taxpayers' expense. The permanent removal of commercial parcels from the tax rolls. The elevated public safety costs on adjacent corridors.

The city is transparent about the costs of its solutions. It has not been transparent about its role in creating the problem those solutions address.

The public comment process, nominally the mechanism through which residents could register dissent, was subject to its own form of capture. Before the pandemic, organized advocacy groups routinely turned out supporters to fill comment periods — but physical attendance imposed a real cost that limited participation to those with strong enough motivation to show up. The normalization of Zoom participation during COVID-19 removed that friction. Public comment could now be submitted from anywhere, by anyone, with no residency verification and no mechanism for the council to distinguish a Berkeley resident bearing the costs of a siting decision from an advocate in another city lending their voice to a cause. The ratio of comments supporting a shelter expansion to comments opposing it was treated as a signal of community sentiment; there was no basis for that assumption. The same advocacy networks that litigated against enforcement, staffed the expert panels, and contracted to operate facilities also organized public comment campaigns. The council saw a queue of supportive voices and recorded it as consensus.

The structure of the meetings themselves reinforced this dynamic. Berkeley City Council meetings routinely run four to five hours, often later, with agendas that bury contested items and comment periods that reward endurance over relevance. For a resident with a job, a commute, or children to put to bed, attending a council meeting to oppose a siting decision is a significant personal cost with uncertain return. For a paid advocate, an ideologically committed activist, or an organization whose contract renewal depends on the outcome, the same five hours is a professional obligation. The asymmetry in who can afford to participate is structural, not incidental — and its effect on the composition of public comment, and therefore on what the council understands as "community input," is systematic.

## A Case Study in Political Incentives: Jesse Arreguín

<div class="key-insight"><p>Arreguín chaired the Rent Board that keeps rents artificially low for incumbents, then championed $110/day motel leases as the city's housing response. He benefited personally from the housing scarcity his policies helped maintain, and directed public spending toward the most expensive possible alternative to resolving it.</p></div>

The trajectory of Jesse Arreguín's career illustrates how Berkeley's policy model reproduced itself over time.

Arreguín represented District 4 on the City Council from 2008 to 2016 — the district that directly abuts the University Avenue corridor where the motel conversions later occurred. The policy framework that produced those conversions can be traced not to a single moment but to a pattern that was set before any of them — beginning in the autumn of 2011.

As Occupy encampments were cleared from Oakland, San Francisco, and cities across the country, Occupy Berkeley established itself in Civic Center Park — directly across the street from Berkeley High School and adjacent to City Hall. In the two months it stood, Berkeley police recorded 33 calls for service, 24 of which were classified as crimes: an attempted rape, a stabbing, multiple assaults, weapons possession, a machete, a handgun, drug sales, and an active investigation into a reported teenage prostitution ring operating from within the camp.<sup><a href="#fn29">29</a></sup> Arreguín, whose district included the park, responded with explicit endorsement. "We don't have any plans to clear people out of the park," he told Berkeleyside. "Our response to Occupy Berkeley represents the city's values. We're a compassionate city."<sup><a href="#fn30">30</a></sup> When the crime rate became publicly impossible to ignore, he proposed a "zero tolerance" policy for rule violations — explicitly exempting overnight camping itself — and expressed hope for a voluntary transition to daytime-only demonstration. The camp was cleared in late December 2011, leaving the park damaged enough to require publicly funded restoration. The cost of police overtime, parks department hazmat cleanup, and grounds restoration was never publicly tallied.

A tent, once inhabited, also acquires a legal dimension Berkeley's political class found convenient not to examine: it constitutes a temporary dwelling for Fourth Amendment purposes, meaning police cannot enter without a warrant. Drug dealing conducted inside an encampment tent is structurally beyond routine enforcement authority. The encampment that formed around political demonstration became a physical structure with constitutional protections. That logic would govern Berkeley's options for the next decade.

That template — declare compassion, defer enforcement, absorb the downstream costs without accounting for them — would repeat itself at increasing expense and in increasingly permanent form. Occupy Berkeley was the proof of concept.

**Measure S** — Berkeley's "sit/lie" ordinance — was backed by Mayor Tom Bates and the Downtown Berkeley Association as a modest tool to move people off sidewalks and toward services. Arreguín led the opposition, framing it as a civil rights violation and a "people mover" that would hide poverty rather than address it. The measure failed, 52% to 48%.

The costs of what that defeat preserved were documented in real time by someone with standing to know. Craig Becker, owner of the Caffe Mediterraneum on Telegraph Avenue and a sitting member of Berkeley's Homeless Commission, published a pro-Measure S op-ed that recorded a before-and-after the opposition never engaged. His business had grown at over 30% annually until the fall of 2009, when a group of nomadic youth established a sidewalk encampment a block away. The growth rate turned immediately and durably negative. Neighboring businesses closed; the owner of Tienda Ho — a 40-year Berkeley institution with locations in both Berkeley and Santa Barbara — told him she was keeping Santa Barbara and closing Berkeley because her best customers had stopped coming to Telegraph. None of this, Becker documented, was attributable to the general economy: nothing had changed except the encampment. The travelers occupying the sidewalk were, he wrote, explicitly service-resistant nomads moving the West Coast circuit who declined outreach by choice — the same population the context document describes. By 2012, he noted, over 60 American cities had passed civil sidewalk ordinances, starting with Seattle in 1993; progressive peer cities including Santa Monica and Santa Cruz had done so successfully using an ambassador-first engagement model that prioritized diversion over citation. Berkeley was choosing a different path.<sup><a href="#fn32">32</a></sup>

The defeat was not merely a policy outcome; it was a political inflection point that transferred initiative on homelessness from the moderate Bates bloc to Arreguín's coalition of service providers, housing advocates, and civil liberties organizations — the coalition he would ride to the mayoralty four years later.

More consequentially, the Measure S fight established a legal and political doctrine that governed Berkeley's options for the next decade: enforcement requires an indoor alternative meeting specific quality standards, and the adequacy of that alternative is determined not by the city but by the advocates and courts that opposed enforcement in the first place. That doctrine, developed in 2012 as a political argument, hardened over the following years into litigation strategy and, eventually, into case law. By the time Berkeley sought to clear the Here/There encampment, the Ashby Shell, and the Harrison corridor, the "predicate" the doctrine required was a private motel room at $110 per night. Arreguín had spent a decade building the political and legal structure that made that price point unavoidable — then authorized the spending to meet it.

During this period he was also a visible supporter of the Here/There encampment at the Berkeley-Oakland border and the Save the Post Office camp, publicly breaking with city policy by stating that if an encampment was "doing a good job and keeping it clean," he did not see why the city should move it. That posture built a reliable constituency among encampment residents, service providers, and housing advocates.

Three years after Measure S, the pattern repeated itself in miniature. In early 2015, Arreguín co-sponsored a proposal with Councilmember Linda Maio asking the city manager to examine a modest set of behavioral standards for downtown: no bedding deployed on sidewalks during daytime hours, no cooking on sidewalks, review of public urination law, prevention of personal property occupying planters and tree wells. The proposal was explicitly not Measure S — it addressed conduct, not status, and included no prohibition on sitting. Within days of the item being noticed, the same activist-legal coalition that had defeated Measure S mobilized against it under the name "The Streets Are for Everyone." Arreguín withdrew his support. "I originally thought that adopting laws and increasing enforcement was going to be the best approach," he explained, "but in thinking more about it I really think without talking about services and outreach … we are not going to make a meaningful difference."<sup><a href="#fn31">31</a></sup> The proposal died. The activists who organized to stop it from being studied are the same network that would later staff the Homeless Services Panel of Experts created by Measure P.

The Save the Post Office camp had a specific genealogy. Mike Zint — who organized under the name "First They Came for the Homeless," a direct appropriation of Pastor Niemöller's poem about Nazi and Soviet murder — had followed the Occupy Berkeley → Civic Center Park → Poor Tour arc to a position in front of the federal post office. Arreguín had his own separate political opposition to the building's proposed sale, and the alignment of their positions gave the encampment a degree of official cover it would not otherwise have had. Two distinct agendas converged on the same piece of public real estate, each lending the other legitimacy.

Over years of outreach, the city offered Zint shelter placements repeatedly. He declined each on grounds of location preference. He eventually accepted a placement and died of the progressive lung disease that had originally made him homeless — a condition caused by decades of tobacco use, which he had framed throughout as evidence of the city's failure to care for its most vulnerable. That framing was taken seriously by Berkeley's political class and amplified by its media. The arc is a precise inversion of Guy Lee's analysis: where Lee diagnosed the system as manufacturing permanent homelessness to justify itself, Zint's public persona depended on homelessness continuing — and the institutional apparatus Arreguín had helped build gave that persona a platform and a measure of political protection it would not otherwise have had.

One resident of Here/There during this period offered the sharpest available critique of what that posture was producing. Guy "Mike" Lee — who had lived outdoors in Berkeley for years without employment, and had family in Las Vegas he declined to contact for help — ran for mayor against Arreguín in the 2016 election under the platform "Old Bum for Mayor." In interviews, Lee described Berkeley's service apparatus as run by "poverty pimps" — organizations that sustained their own contracts by managing homelessness indefinitely rather than ending it. He argued for "a hand up and not a hand-out," proposed converting unused city space into modest senior housing where residents would pay nominal rent from their Social Security income, and identified homeless youth as a distinct emergency requiring direct resources rather than navigation center intake. His diagnosis of the existing system was specific: "The way we deal with homelessness now is a broken system — there is no exit strategy. There is basically someone handing you a sandwich, a mat on the floor and a little pat on the head."<sup><a href="#fn23">23</a></sup> Arreguín won the election. Lee died without being housed. The system Lee criticized has, by every financial measure, continued to expand.

He was elected mayor in 2016 and served through 2024, a period during which Berkeley developed its "compassionate enforcement" framework. That framework produced the STAIR navigation center and, eventually, the motel master leases — including the Super 8 conversion at $110 per room per night — which were explicitly designed to provide an indoor alternative for encampment residents before any clearing action could proceed. The non-congregate model those leases reflect, mandating single-occupancy rooms rather than lower-cost congregate shelter, was a direct product of the activist pressure his earlier posture had encouraged and legitimized.

In his final months as mayor, following the U.S. Supreme Court's *Grants Pass v. Johnson* ruling in June 2024 — which restored local authority to enforce anti-camping ordinances — Arreguín authorized the City Manager to begin clearing long-standing encampments, using the motel rooms as the required alternative. The $110/day infrastructure he had helped create became the legal predicate for the enforcement action he had spent years deferring.

He was elected to the State Senate in November 2024. He now chairs the Senate Committee on Housing and the Senate Committee on Public Safety. The doctrine he developed in Berkeley — enforce nothing without a premium alternative, define the alternative's adequacy through advocacy rather than cost discipline — is now available to him at the scale of California's entire homelessness and public safety budget.

Throughout this period, Arreguín has resided in the same rent-controlled apartment he occupied as a UC Berkeley student, paying a reported $624 per month — roughly $20 per day — for a one-bedroom in Downtown Berkeley at a time when market rents for comparable units ran $2,500–$3,000 per month. The figure was reported by Berkeleyside during his 2016 mayoral campaign. Before his council tenure he chaired the Berkeley Rent Stabilization Board, the body that administers the system from which he personally benefits.

Rent control functions as a personal benefit only in a housing market where supply remains constrained relative to demand. Arreguín's $20/day apartment exists because Berkeley has not built enough housing to close that gap. The motel conversions he championed cost the city $110/day per room — more than five times his own housing cost — in part because the non-congregate mandate his political environment enforced ruled out lower-cost alternatives. His background story, a family displaced by eviction in San Francisco, became the public rationale for that premium spending. The structural irony went largely unexamined: a city official who benefited from housing scarcity, administered the rules that perpetuate it, and then directed public funds into the most expensive possible response to its consequences.

The point is not that his positions were insincere. It is that his personal circumstances, his political constituency, and his policy preferences were in alignment for sixteen years, producing outcomes that were foreseeable and that Berkeley's residents and taxpayers are now paying for.

The pattern of ignoring expert advice and absorbing the consequences with public money was not limited to homelessness programs. In 2017, the Arreguín administration used $3.1 million from Berkeley's general fund to purchase the former **Premier Cru** building at 1011 University Avenue — a 10,000-square-foot property named after a wine retailer that had collapsed under a federal Ponzi scheme prosecution. The stated purpose was a civic annex: new council chambers and city offices, part of a plan to move functions out of the seismically compromised Old City Hall. City staff raised immediate warnings. The building required a gurney-sized ADA-compliant elevator to legally host public meetings on its second floor, and the structural configuration made installing one prohibitively expensive — estimates for the seismic retrofit and ADA work were approaching double the purchase price. The council approved the acquisition anyway.

The building never served its intended purpose. After the renovation costs made the civic annex plan untenable, the city leased the space to Lifelong Medical Care and associated partners as a hub for homeless services and medical respite, at a below-market rate. A $3.1 million general-fund acquisition on a prime University Avenue parcel — a commercial property that had been generating property tax revenue — became subsidized real estate for a nonprofit service provider. The parcel left the tax rolls. The services provided are real; the real estate transaction that houses them was a failure of due diligence that the city absorbed rather than acknowledged. The building is widely regarded as a white elephant: it failed every metric of the acquisition that justified the purchase, and the sunk cost has been managed rather than resolved.

At 1011 University, the Premier Cru building joins the converted motels at 1461, 1619, and 1761 University Avenue as another point on a corridor studded with homeless services. University Avenue is Berkeley's primary western approach to one of the world's great public universities. It is difficult to identify another major university city — Ann Arbor, Cambridge, New Haven, Chapel Hill, Madison — that has made comparable land-use choices on its principal approach to campus.

## The Specialized Care Unit: Another Pilot That Couldn't Fail

<div class="key-insight"><p>Berkeley had a functioning mobile crisis team for fifty years before the SCU existed. Anti-police politics made the co-response model it depended on politically untenable. The SCU was built alongside it as an alternative rather than an improvement — and the integration that might have made both programs work never happened, because the political coalition that demanded the SCU had no interest in a model that included police. The county was legally obligated to provide mobile crisis services all along. Berkeley spent $4.5 million to replicate something it was entitled to for free, produced an inferior version, and has not advocated to collect what the original entitlement required.</p></div>

Berkeley's Mobile Crisis Team had operated since the 1970s — a co-response model pairing licensed clinicians from the city's Mental Health Division with Berkeley Police Department officers. The team carried real legal authority the SCU would never have: clinicians could initiate 5150 involuntary holds, a power that civilian responders cannot exercise. For five decades the MCT was the city's only mental health crisis resource, and it worked in the sense that matters — it showed up, it had legal tools, it handled the calls it was sent to.

The 2013 death of Kayla Moore during a police encounter became the catalytic event for a decade of advocacy demanding the co-response model's abolition. Activists argued that police were the wrong tool for mental health crises and that clinical response required complete separation from law enforcement. The argument was not resolved by evidence — co-response models in other jurisdictions had documented records of effectiveness — but by political energy. The George Floyd protests in summer 2020 gave that argument a majority on the council. In July 2020, Berkeley City Council passed a resolution to create a civilian-led Specialized Care Unit that would respond to crises without any police involvement.<sup><a href="#fn16">16</a></sup>

The SCU was designed not to integrate with the MCT but to replace the function the MCT's critics found objectionable — the police involvement. In practice, the two programs ran on parallel tracks with minimal coordination. The MCT was dispatched via police radio; the SCU for its first year of operation ran on a separate phone line disconnected from 911 dispatch, meaning most callers in crisis never reached it. Any call involving mention of a weapon — including a pocketknife — automatically excluded SCU response and reverted to the MCT and BPD. The result was a de facto division of the city's crisis population: the SCU handled low-to-moderate acuity calls involving unhoused or visibly distressed individuals in accessible situations; the MCT retained everything that carried legal or safety complexity. The integration that might have made both programs more effective never occurred, because the political constituency that demanded the SCU had built it specifically as an alternative to the MCT's model, not a complement to it.

The program contracted with Resource Development Associates to design the model and Bonita House to operate it, funded by approximately $4.5 million in one-time federal American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA) dollars. Like the STAIR Center before it, it was framed as a pilot — and like STAIR, it was built from the outset with permanent-level staffing costs and specialized vehicles, with 24/7 coverage as the stated goal.

The operational reality fell well short. When the SCU launched in September 2023, it operated only during daytime hours — roughly 8:00 a.m. to 4:00 p.m. By mid-2024 the unit had expanded to 16 hours a day. The graveyard shift was never filled. The model required each team to include a licensed clinician, a peer support specialist, and an EMT simultaneously — a staffing combination that proved difficult to assemble and impossible to sustain at night, when crisis calls peak. Berkeley was competing for the same small pool of behavioral health workers as Oakland's MACRO program and regional hospitals, in a market where mobile crisis fieldwork commands a wage premium the city's budget could not meet.

The SCU failed as a police alternative on two independent axes, either of which would have been sufficient. The first was coverage: for at least the first year of operation, and for some portion of every night throughout the pilot, the SCU was not operating. Berkeley Police handled those hours exactly as before. The program never eliminated a single shift of police response to behavioral health calls — it added a parallel service during the hours it could staff.

The second failure was design: a civilian unit encountering a person in crisis has no authority and no capacity when that person becomes violent. At the first sign of danger, a police call was required regardless. The SCU was therefore a supplement to policing even during its operational hours, not a substitute. Both failures ran simultaneously and independently.

During the pilot period, the unit handled approximately 1,000 calls — at a cost of roughly $4,500 per call against the ARPA budget alone, before adding the consultant fees paid to Resource Development Associates to design the model. No failure threshold was established, and no per-call cost target was ever published.

In April 2025, City Manager Paul Buddenhagen recommended transitioning the SCU to Alameda County Behavioral Health Care Services.<sup><a href="#fn17">17</a></sup> The official framing was a "strategic transition" to align with California's Cal-AIM mandate, which, effective January 1, 2023, required counties to implement Community-Based Mobile Crisis Intervention Services as a Medi-Cal benefit — making the county the permanent funding conduit for exactly this type of program. That mandate had been in place since the SCU launched. The county was going to run a mobile crisis program regardless of what Berkeley did. Berkeley's $4.5 million ARPA expenditure purchased a 16-month partial-coverage pilot for a service the county was legally obligated to provide anyway, then declared the handoff a success.

The obligation runs deeper than Cal-AIM. Under the terms of a litigation settlement in *Disability Rights California v. Alameda County*, the county is separately required to provide mobile crisis response, peer respites, and crisis residential services countywide. The settlement creates an entitlement: Berkeley is owed its proportionate share of county mobile crisis capacity as a matter of law. At the April 2026 Budget Committee hearing, Carole Marasovic — a member of the Homeless Services Panel of Experts — stated publicly that Berkeley has not been advocating to claim that share, and that other jurisdictions have received allocations Berkeley has not pursued. The city dismantled a working program, spent $4.5 million building an inferior replacement, handed the replacement to the county, and has not collected the services the county is legally required to provide. The net position, relative to doing nothing, is a functional mobile crisis deficit.

The fact that this indictment came from inside the Panel of Experts is notable. The Panel's composition — drawn from the same advocacy community that opposed co-response policing — reflects the same political coalition that made the Mobile Crisis Team's model untenable in the first place. That one of its members named the anti-police sentiment as the mechanism of failure, on the record, at a Budget Committee hearing, is a departure from the panel's institutional posture. Whether that represents a genuine accountability inflection or an isolated dissent remains to be seen.

The SCU's arc is the STAIR arc compressed into a shorter timeline: a politically motivated pilot with no failure criteria, high per-unit costs, and an exit that produced no durable infrastructure. The primary legacy is the expenditure. The Mobile Crisis Team that predated it still exists, reduced by chronic understaffing and neglect to one or two active clinicians — a program that had served the city for fifty years and received no investment during the same period the SCU consumed $4.5 million. Berkeley ends the experiment with a depleted co-response team it refused to strengthen, an outsourced civilian unit that lost its local identity on the way to the county, and an entitlement under the *DRC v. Alameda County* settlement it has not claimed.

## The City's Own Auditor Confirms the System's Dysfunction

<div class="key-insight"><p>Berkeley spent $35 million on homelessness services in FY2024 and produced a public record that a performance auditor could not use to determine whether the program improved, held steady, or declined. The coordination meetings stopped for a year. Street outreach staff couldn't make shelter referrals for three years. Demand rose as the count fell. The city agreed with every finding.</p></div>

The July 2025 audit of the Homeless Response Team by Berkeley City Auditor Jenny Wong covered HRT operations for fiscal years 2022 through 2024.<sup><a href="#fn39">39</a></sup> It confirmed, in the city's own official record, several patterns this analysis documents from the outside.

**The structural gap.** At the time of the audit, Berkeley had 844 unhoused residents and 320 shelter beds — a ratio the auditor presented as the operational context for everything the HRT does. Of those 320 beds, the HRT controls 56. The remaining 264 are administered by Alameda County's Continuum of Care. The city agency tasked with connecting people to shelter controls fewer than one in five of the beds it is supposed to fill.

**Three years without the ability to make referrals.** The HRT launched in September 2021. It did not gain access to the Homeless Management Information System — the data platform all Alameda County providers use — until December 2022, fifteen months in. It could not conduct Coordinated Entry assessments — the formal intake process required to connect a client to a county-administered shelter bed — until September 2024, three years after launch. Before that access existed, roughly half of all client interactions required staff to route the client to a separate organization just to complete an intake. The auditor found this structural gap "may have increased wait times to access resources." The effect was more fundamental: for three of its first four years of operation, Berkeley's street outreach team could not place a client in a county shelter without involving a third party. The coordination that was the program's stated purpose was not operationally possible.

**A year without coordination.** From October 2023 to September 2024, the HRT suspended its regular case conference meetings with service providers — the primary mechanism for cross-organizational coordination — due to litigation. The auditor found that providers lost track of clients during this period. This is not a data quality issue. For a full year, the team responsible for coordinating homeless services stopped coordinating.

**A demand signal that refused to fall.** Despite the unsheltered count declining following the Supreme Court's *Grants Pass* ruling and the Arreguín clearances of 2024, 311 calls to the HRT increased — from 880 to 1,090. The auditor explicitly flagged this as unexplained, and recommended that HRT track and report on service demand in ways that support trend analysis. The city's own accountability mechanism recorded rising service demand at exactly the moment the political class was presenting the population count's decline as evidence of success.

**Outcomes measured in ways that prevent accountability.** All reported HRT outcomes — 655 shelter offers, 303 acceptances, 59 encampment closures, one million pounds of trash removed, 1,405 encampment notices issued — were presented cumulatively from program inception, not by reporting period. The auditor found it was therefore impossible to evaluate progress toward goals or identify trends over time. The figure most commonly cited — that 20% of HRT clients exit to permanent housing — is a cumulative total since 2021 and cannot be disaggregated by year, quarter, or cohort. The auditor separately could not independently verify the numbers because HRT had stored most data in Excel spreadsheets rather than shared systems, and gaps in the internal data were observable. The program also did not track why people declined shelter offers — information the auditor identified as essential to understanding whether the system's constraints are logistical or motivational. Berkeley spent $35 million on homelessness services in FY2024 and produced a public record from which no trend could be extracted.

**The staffing comparison the city did not make.** Oakland's Encampment Management Team — a city with a comparable approach — operated at the time of the audit with 7 dedicated FTE in the City Administrator's Office plus dedicated personnel from Public Works (10 staff), Police (2), Fire (1), and Human Services (3), working exclusively on encampment operations and data. Berkeley's HRT had 3–4 FTE. A staffing analysis commissioned by the city in 2024 recommended adding an administrative position; the Council agreed and added one FTE in the FY2026 Mid-biennial budget. The Homeless Program Manager position in HHCS has since been retired, and that vacancy frozen — as part of the same budget that is now proposing to eliminate 32 police positions. The program that generates the calls consuming patrol capacity is managed by a team the city's own consultant found inadequate.

City management agreed with every finding. Implementation dates are primarily September 1, 2025 — with one notable exception. Integrating shared data systems into provider contracts cannot happen until the next contract renewal cycle; contracts were renewed in 2024 on four-year terms, which means the next regular opportunity is August 1, 2028. Berkeley's homelessness coordination infrastructure will not be coherently connected to provider data systems for at least three more years.<sup><a href="#fn39">39</a></sup>

## Cumulative Effect

The cumulative effect was a city that often signaled tolerance, subsidized response systems, constrained enforcement, and redirected public resources while underinvesting in baseline municipal functions. Encampments such as Here/There, the marina RV community, and the Harrison corridor were not isolated surprises. They were foreseeable outcomes of a policy model that combined empathy-driven politics with weak boundary setting and inconsistent cost discipline.

The population count is the most direct measure of whether the system produced its stated outcome. In 2009, Berkeley's point-in-time homeless count stood at 683 — 312 sheltered, 371 unsheltered — with the city spending around $3 million annually on services.<sup><a href="#fn31">31</a></sup> Over the following decade, that spending grew by orders of magnitude through Measure P, ARPA, state grants, federal allocations, motel master leases, and the full ballot measure stack. The count grew with it.<sup><a href="#fn35">35</a></sup>

| Year | Sheltered | Unsheltered | Total | Change |
|------|-----------|-------------|-------|--------|
| 2009 | 312 | 371 | 683 | baseline |
| 2015 | 266 | 568 | 834 | +22% |
| 2017 | 308 | 664 | 972 | +16% |
| 2019 | 295 | 813 | 1,108 | +14% |
| 2022 | 254 | 803 | 1,057 | −5% |
| 2024 | 399 | 445 | 844 | −20% |

The count peaked at 1,108 in 2019 — a 62% increase from 2009 — across the entire arc of Berkeley's non-enforcement and service-expansion era. The 2022 figure shows an essentially flat −5% that Berkeley cited as evidence of progress. The 2024 figure of 844 reflects a more substantial −20% decline from the 2022 count, achieved following the U.S. Supreme Court's *Grants Pass v. Johnson* ruling and the subsequent encampment clearances Arreguín authorized in his final months as mayor.

What the top-line 2024 figure conceals is the mechanism. Unsheltered fell by 358 (from 803 to 445); sheltered *rose* by 145 (from 254 to 399). The "decline" was almost entirely a movement of people from encampments into the motel and shelter infrastructure — the $110/day rooms built as clearing predicates. It was not an exit from homelessness. Of the 844 people counted on a given night in 2024, 399 were in shelter — a net increase of only 87 over the 312 sheltered in 2009, after fifteen years of ballot measure investment in shelter capacity. Unsheltered Berkeley, at 445, remained 20% above 2009's 371.

The city's own July 2024 Homeless System Gap Analysis confirms the system dynamics behind these counts.<sup><a href="#fn36">36</a></sup> The annual unduplicated count — how many different people experienced homelessness over the full year — was 2,100 in 2022, roughly double the single-night figure, reflecting significant population turnover. Total people accessing Berkeley's homelessness services grew from 1,813 in 2021 to 2,332 in 2023. Shelter capacity was essentially flat; the entire growth was in non-shelter service users. The gap analysis titled one of its summary slides "More people are entering homelessness system than exiting" — 650 newly homeless plus 100 returning against 500 exiting to housing, a net annual increase of approximately 250. The city producing that analysis had spent fifteen years building a system explicitly designed to end homelessness.

The city that congratulated itself on reducing homelessness was comparing to a peak its own policies had produced — and measuring a decline driven by enforcement authority it had spent a decade opposing. The 2009 baseline of 683 has never been recovered. The structural reason is not mysterious: a system with no residency requirements, premium services, and no failure criteria does not reduce the population it serves. It attracts the population that justifies its existence.

Berkeley's recent shift toward firmer enforcement and more candid discussion of regional inflow suggests city leadership has begun to recognize those dynamics. But for many years, the city's own decisions were central to the conditions it later described as external crises.

## The Succession: Harrison and Tregub

<div class="key-insight"><p>District 4 has been represented since 2008 by officials with the same regulatory background, the same political constituency, and the same policy preferences. The seat changes; the model does not. That is not coincidence — it reflects a coalition whose interests are served by continuity.</p></div>

Arreguín's District 4 seat did not change character when he left for the mayor's office. His successor, **Kate Harrison**, won a special election in 2017 and represented the district through 2024.<sup><a href="#fn7">7</a></sup> Where Arreguín built his political identity through encampment advocacy and the Rent Board, Harrison arrived with a background in public administration and fiscal policy — and applied that expertise to building the tax and governance infrastructure the advocacy model required. She was a primary author of Measure P and, with council colleagues, the architect of the Homeless Services Panel of Experts embedded within it, creating the financial pipeline and institutional framework that converted political commitments into binding city spending.<sup><a href="#fn5">5</a></sup> The non-congregate standard — private rooms with locking doors as the only "humane" alternative — was a position she championed, though it commanded majority council support; it locked the city into a price point of roughly $3,300 per month per unit, approaching the cost of market-rate studio apartments in Berkeley.

The clearest expression of that gap is her own timeline around the Hope Center. Harrison had championed the Berkeley Way project from the 2013 council vote to get started without a funding plan through the September 2021 construction walkthrough she led for stakeholders. At the October 2022 ribbon-cutting — standing alongside Mayor Arreguín, State Senator Nancy Skinner, and officials from the California Department of Housing and Community Development — she described the $120 million facility as "a complete win-win" for Berkeley.<sup><a href="#fn37">37</a></sup> Fifteen months later, she resigned from the council, describing city government as "broken" and "byzantine."

Harrison was also, within the limits of how Berkeley's council operates, genuinely frustrated by the city management's inability to deliver on those commitments. The dysfunction she named when she resigned — describing city government as "broken" and "byzantine" — was not a parting shot or a rhetorical exit: it reflected a real tension she had encountered between political ambition and administrative capacity.<sup><a href="#fn8">8</a></sup> The accountability she wanted from city staff was real. The disagreement with this analysis is not about her intentions; it is about whether the policy architecture she helped build was capable of producing accountability regardless of who wanted it. The Hope Center, which she celebrated as proof of what the system could do, serves 97 people at a capital cost of $738,000 per unit. The University Avenue shelter corridor that accumulated in the same district she represented served far more people, at lower cost, with no ribbon-cutting and no officials from Sacramento.

**Igor Tregub** won the subsequent special election for the seat.<sup><a href="#fn9">9</a></sup> His background tracks Arreguín's almost precisely: years on the Berkeley Rent Stabilization Board including a stint as chair, the Housing Advisory Commission, and the Planning Commission. His entry into District 4 politics carries its own timing question: Tregub moved into the district roughly a year before it became apparent Harrison would not seek re-election — the kind of relocation that, in Berkeley political circles, is understood to be preparation for a run, not a coincidence of residential preference. During his campaign he explicitly named the dysfunction he intended to fix: "I've seen a really big problem with the culture of City Hall, both from the City Council and senior management, that basically views the public and public processes as a distraction from their more enlightened work, rather than as the fundamental job of the city." He campaigned on transparency, accountability, and revitalizing downtown Berkeley — the corridor directly affected by the shelter cluster his district contains.<sup><a href="#fn20">20</a></sup>

The distinction matters. Harrison expressed her frustrations and left. Tregub ran on naming the same dysfunction and then, in office, reproduced it. The 1761 University Ave interim housing facility opened in February 2026 — during Tregub's tenure — before the surrounding community had been meaningfully consulted on the siting decision. The pattern he described as a pathology of city governance was applied to the corridor he had specifically pledged to revitalize. His regulatory background — the Rent Board, the Zoning Adjustments Board, the Planning Commission — reinforces rather than challenges the conditions that make $110/day motel leases the path of least resistance. When new permanent supportive housing costs $738,044 per unit to build in Berkeley — the documented figure for the Hope Center, Berkeley's own flagship project — leasing a Super 8 room for $40,000 a year becomes a politically presentable alternative.

District 4 has been represented continuously since 2008 by officials whose personal housing situations, political constituencies, and policy preferences point in the same direction. That consistency is not coincidence — it reflects a durable political coalition whose interests are served by the current model.

## Fire as Cost and Pretext

<div class="key-insight"><p>Residents who created the greatest public safety hazards received the highest-priority, highest-cost placements. Those who caused less disruption waited longer for less. Whatever the intent, the operational incentive structure rewarded escalation over compliance.</p></div>

Encampment and RV fires imposed direct costs on Berkeley that have never been aggregated in public accounting. Berkeley Fire Department units responded repeatedly to blazes at the Harrison Street corridor, the Ashby Avenue interchange, the University Avenue freeway on-ramps, and the Here/There site. Fires involving propane tanks — common in long-term RV encampments — posed risks to neighboring structures and Caltrans infrastructure. Each response consumed fire department resources; major fires drew multiple units and generated hours of overtime.<sup><a href="#fn18">18</a></sup>

These fires also functioned as political and legal instruments. Under the frameworks established by *Martin v. Boise* and later *Grants Pass v. Johnson*, cities faced constraints on clearing encampments unless they could demonstrate specific health or safety hazards. "Fire life safety" became the most reliable predicate — a documented fire at a site gave the city grounds to act that softer arguments about neighborhood impact did not. The pattern was consistent: a significant fire would be followed by a council meeting or Homeless Services Panel session at which the danger of encampments was invoked to justify renewed or expanded motel lease funding.

The Ashby Shell encampment at the I-80 interchange became the sharpest illustration of how this dynamic played out. The site had housed individuals for years — in one documented case, a resident who had been living on Berkeley streets for 29 years.<sup><a href="#fn19">19</a></sup> As the encampment became more entrenched, residents tapped into Caltrans electrical infrastructure — freeway signage and utility boxes — to power cooking equipment, heaters, and charging stations inside tents and RVs. The resulting arc flashes and electrical fires gave Berkeley Fire Department the specific "life safety" documentation needed to clear the site under emergency protocols, bypassing the extended administrative process that litigation by encampment-aligned legal organizations had otherwise made necessary.<sup><a href="#fn19">19</a></sup>

The residents cleared from Ashby were transitioned into the Rodeway Inn on University Avenue under a separate master lease, on the same general model as the Super 8 — private rooms, on-site services, Measure P funding. The 29-year resident was cited by city officials as validation of the non-congregate approach: only a private room, the argument went, could offer a realistic path indoors for someone with that history of outdoor living.

The characterization of the 29-year resident as evidence for non-congregate housing requirements deserves scrutiny. Berkeley's housing market has been expensive for decades, but it has not been uniformly inaccessible. Over three decades — spanning housing downturns, periods of SRO availability, nonprofit transitional programs, federal voucher cycles, and the city's own expanding shelter capacity — someone motivated to live indoors and capable of sustaining tenancy had multiple plausible paths to do so. Tenure of that length is evidence not primarily of housing unavailability but of severe mental illness, substance dependence, or what service providers call "service resistance" — a documented pattern of declining offered interventions. None of those conditions are addressed by a private motel room. The irony runs deeper: the non-congregate mandate that drove the $110/day lease price was itself justified by the argument that this population would only accept a private room. But an individual who has declined every intervention for three decades is not demonstrating a preference for single-occupancy rooms over congregate shelter. They are demonstrating that they do not want indoor housing on any terms the city has offered. The accommodation of that preference at $146 per bed per day was presented as compassion; it was also a very expensive wager against the available evidence.

The other residents cleared from Ashby presented similar profiles. The shortest outdoor tenure among those documented was approximately five years. This is not a population characterized by recent misfortune or an unlucky encounter with a tight rental market.

The policy implication extends well beyond the Ashby case. Point-in-time counts of "unsheltered homeless" include individuals like these alongside recently displaced people for whom housing access is the primary unmet need. Those counts are the evidentiary basis cited by advocates and policymakers to justify more construction, more leases, more service contracts. When the count includes substantial numbers of long-tenure, treatment-resistant individuals, the implied policy response — build more units, lease more rooms — is calibrated to a different population than the one actually counted. The housing gets built; those individuals remain outside or cycle through placements without stabilizing; the count does not fall; the call for more housing continues. The feedback loop is internally consistent only if outdoor tenure is attributed entirely to supply constraints — an assumption the Ashby population does not support, and that Berkeley never tested.

The structural incentive this created went unexamined. The encampment population that generated the most severe hazards — electrical theft, recurring fires, infrastructure damage — received the highest-cost housing placements. Those who had caused less disruption in less visible locations received less. The system rewarded entrenchment and escalation, not compliance or cooperation, and did so with no public accounting of the total cost from first fire response to final motel placement.

## The Pilot-to-Permanent Pipeline: STAIR as Prototype

<div class="key-insight"><p>A pilot program without defined failure criteria cannot fail. STAIR's renewal was never contingent on placing people in permanent housing — only on continuing to operate. That template was applied to every program that followed.</p></div>

The structural pattern that produced the $110/day motel leases was established earlier, with the STAIR Center navigation center — originally called the Pathways Project — which opened in June 2018 as an explicitly temporary, six-month pilot program.<sup><a href="#fn12">12</a></sup>

The pilot's stated logic was straightforward: move encampment residents (including those at Here/There and Cedar Street, which Arreguín had visited) into the navigation center, then into permanent housing within 90 days. What the City Council and the Homeless Services Panel of Experts did not establish were any defined failure criteria — no minimum housing-exit rate, no maximum cost-per-placement, no threshold below which the program would be discontinued. Because failure was never defined, any level of operation constituted a success worth renewing.

When the six-month period ended in late 2018, the council renewed funding using Measure P and state HEAP (Homeless Emergency Aid Program) dollars, relying on process metrics — bed utilization rates, meals served, low-barrier entry — rather than outcome data.<sup><a href="#fn13">13</a></sup> By the time any outcome data emerged, STAIR had acquired a separate political function: it was the legal predicate for encampment clearings. The city could authorize clearing because STAIR existed as an indoor alternative. That made STAIR operationally untouchable regardless of its housing placement rate.

The program also carried a design commitment the operator did not honor. STAIR was intended to operate with a maximum six-month stay limit — long enough to stabilize a resident and connect them with permanent housing, short enough to turn over capacity for the next encampment residents waiting for a bed. In practice, stays extended well past that limit. When the council did eventually receive operator reports, those reports reflected a selection problem — and not an inadvertent one. BACS, the operator, screened potential residents for what it called "preparedness to engage": because each bed was tied to a Rapid Rehousing subsidy requiring a quick lease signing, the program prioritized individuals with some form of income who were "document ready." The effect was to systematically exclude the chronically homeless individuals with severe mental illness or long-term disabilities who were hardest to place — precisely the population the program was publicly justified by. BACS defended the model as appropriate to a Navigation Center design: the mission was to move people through the system, which required selecting clients capable of succeeding in a 90-day housing search. The defense was coherent as a description of the program's actual function. It was incompatible with the program's stated purpose of serving Berkeley's street population.

The result was initial reported exit rates of 80% or higher — a figure the council and the Homeless Services Panel cited as evidence of the model's success and used to justify continued funding. Local activists and some city officials challenged the number as reflecting "cherry picking" rather than genuine impact. The challenge was accurate. The people STAIR was reporting as exits to housing were largely people who would have found housing with or without the intervention. The people on Berkeley's streets — the population the program's political justification required it to serve — remained there.

Subsequent reviews suggested a significant share of STAIR exits were to "unknown" locations or back to the street. At an operating cost of approximately $2.4 million per year for 45 beds — roughly $146 per bed per day — the implied cost per genuine placement was far higher than the reported 80% exit rate suggested. At the honest exit rate that Berkeley's current higher-acuity facilities produce (40–45%, serving populations that cannot self-resolve), the cost per successful housing exit from STAIR would have been approximately $125,000–$130,000 per person — roughly double what the headline rate implied.<sup><a href="#fn14">14</a></sup> That analysis was never presented publicly.

The shift is visible in the longitudinal data. The Hope Center, operating under Alameda County's Coordinated Entry System with explicit high-acuity targeting, reports exit-to-housing rates of approximately 40–45%. This looks worse than STAIR's claimed 80%. It is more honest. Policy analysts generally read the current lower numbers as evidence that the system is now serving the population it always claimed to serve, rather than the population easiest to move. The cost of that honesty is that the marquee metric — exit rate — declined when the program stopped gaming it.

The STAIR experience established the template the motel leases inherited: spend at a high per-unit rate, measure process rather than outcomes, and ensure the program acquires a political function — clearing camps, satisfying grant conditions, demonstrating "compassion" — that insulates it from performance-based review. The Super 8 lease was authorized without defined success criteria, without a cost-per-exit target, and without a sunset provision contingent on measurable outcomes. It followed the model precisely.

## "Expertise" as Political Cover

The Homeless Services Panel of Experts does not function as independent technical oversight. Its primary institutional role is to provide political legitimacy for spending decisions that have effectively already been made. When the City Council approves a $976,580 lease for a 23-room motel, the panel's prior vetting provides cover: the decision was reviewed by experts. That the experts are appointed by the same council members whose priorities they validate, and that their professional backgrounds are drawn overwhelmingly from the nonprofit service sector with a direct financial stake in continued spending, is not prominently disclosed.

The same structure governs other Berkeley tax measures. The Sugar-Sweetened Beverage Product Panel of Experts, created by the 2014 soda tax ballot measure, requires members with professional backgrounds in nutrition science, public health research, or medical practice — specific credentials defined in the municipal code.<sup><a href="#fn10">10</a></sup> **Adena Ishii**, who won the 2024 mayoral race succeeding Arreguín, cited her appointment to this panel as evidence of "ten years of local policy experience" in her candidate statement. Her professional background is in nonprofit consulting and community organizing, with degrees in business (UC Berkeley Haas) and law (Santa Clara University). The credential requirements of the ordinance she was appointed to administer do not describe her background.<sup><a href="#fn11">11</a></sup>

The pattern across both panels is consistent: appointment to a body with "Expert" in the title functions as a résumé credential and a political exchange — the appointing official gains a reliable ally on the panel; the appointee gains a platform. Independent technical scrutiny of high-cost decisions is not the output. Political legitimacy for those decisions is.
