# Methodology

## Purpose

This project examines whether four converted motel housing sites on University Avenue in Berkeley were followed by changes in nearby non-traffic police calls for service.

The goal is not to debate homelessness policy. The goal is to measure whether nearby public-safety demand changed after specific sites opened.

---

## Sites Studied

- 1461 University Ave
- 1512 University Ave
- 1619 University Ave
- 1761 University Ave

---

## Why Non-Traffic Calls

Traffic stops and collisions happen for many reasons unrelated to nearby housing.

Non-traffic calls are a better signal for neighborhood public-safety impact. These include:

- Disturbance
- Trespassing
- Suspicious person or vehicle
- Welfare check
- Theft
- Mentally ill / 5150 calls
- Encampment complaints
- Assault, battery, vandalism

---

## Before / After Windows

For each site, we identified an approximate opening date.

We then compared:

- **12 months before opening**
- **12 months after opening**

Using the same location before and after helps control for pre-existing neighborhood characteristics — same street, same businesses, same transit access.

---

## Geographic Zones

To measure whether effects spread beyond the site itself, we divided the analysis into three concentric zones:

| Zone | Description | Approximate radius |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| Zone 1 | Site block | 100 m |
| Zone 2 | Adjacent blocks | 300 m |
| Zone 3 | Wider nearby area | 600 m |

If only Zone 1 shows increases, impacts may be contained on-site.
If Zone 2 also increases, nearby residents and businesses may be affected.

---

## Control Corridors

We applied the same analysis to two busy Berkeley commercial corridors:

- **North Shattuck** (Gourmet Ghetto area)
- **South Telegraph**

These areas have comparable pedestrian traffic, shops, and transit. They serve as rough baselines to identify citywide trends that would affect results regardless of housing site activity.

---

## Rolling 3-Month Year-over-Year Comparisons

Some call types vary by season. To reduce seasonal bias, we also computed rolling 3-month windows compared year-over-year.

Example: February–April 2025 compared with February–April 2024.

This separates site-related patterns from annual seasonal cycles.

---

## Measuring Change

We measured percentage change:

> Percentage change = (post-period count − pre-period count) / pre-period count × 100

A result of +50% means 50% more calls in the 12 months after opening compared with the 12 months before.

## Data Sources

---

## Limits

This analysis does **not** prove that any individual call was caused by a housing site.

Other factors that can affect call counts:

- Citywide trends
- Economic conditions
- Police staffing levels
- Weather
- Changes in reporting behavior
- Nearby construction or development

Results show association and timing. They are not proof of causation.

---

## Why This Still Matters

When multiple sites show similar patterns — especially increases in adjacent blocks after opening — that consistency is useful evidence for community discussion, even without proof of causation.

The goal is to move from anecdote to measurable, reproducible findings.
