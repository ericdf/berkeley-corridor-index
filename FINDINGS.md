# Findings: The Public Safety Impact of Shelter Siting on University Avenue and San Pablo Avenue

## Executive Summary

Between 2021 and 2023, the City of Berkeley converted four motels on University Avenue and one on San Pablo Avenue into interim or permanent supportive housing. This analysis uses five years of Berkeley Police Department calls-for-service data, RIPA stop data, and corridor-level benchmarking to measure the public safety consequences of those decisions.

The findings are consistent across multiple independent methods:

- **The shelter corridors diverged sharply from the rest of Berkeley.** Most comparable commercial corridors improved over the study period. University Avenue and San Pablo did not.
- **Call volume increased and the nature of calls shifted** toward categories that directly harm neighboring residents: theft, disturbance, welfare checks, and person-down incidents.
- **Police stops near the sites more than doubled.** Stops near 1461 University Ave rose 164% post-opening; stops near the Golden Bear Inn on San Pablo rose 186%.
- **The stop population near 1461 is disproportionately non-Berkeley residents** — 20.8% vs. a citywide baseline of 14.5% — consistent with a county placement pipeline that exports costs to Berkeley taxpayers.
- **The impact extends beyond the immediate sites.** The Hope Center at 2012 Berkeley Way triggered a step-change on North Shattuck in mid-2022, producing a sustained ~70% increase in calls that has not subsided.
- **The full fiscal cost has not been tallied.** The 1619 University Ave lease alone totals $5.18M over five years before service contracts, with state grant coverage expiring after year 2. Matching obligations, litigation costs, and permanent property tax attrition have never been presented to residents in an integrated accounting.

These findings almost certainly understate the true impact. The study period coincides with documented reductions in both proactive policing and citizen reporting in Berkeley. Increases occurred against that headwind.

## Background: The Opportunity Cost

University Avenue is Berkeley's primary western approach to one of the world's great public universities. It is also a corridor that has struggled commercially for years — but not uniformly. East of Martin Luther King Jr. Way, the Trader Joe's development (a motel-to-retail redevelopment) transformed a blighted strip mall and generated measurable improvement in foot traffic and surrounding business activity. That project demonstrates what intentional redevelopment on this corridor can produce.

West of MLK, the city made a different choice. Rather than pursuing redevelopment of distressed motels, it converted them into shelter and supportive housing — concentrating multiple facilities within a half-mile stretch. The five sites studied here represent that policy:

| Site | Address | Type | Opened |
|------|---------|------|--------|
| 1461 University Ave | University Ave | Interim housing / shelter | July 2021 |
| 1619 University Ave | University Ave | Reentry / supportive housing | July 2023 |
| 1761 University Ave | University Ave | Interim housing | February 2026 |
| Golden Bear Inn | 1620 San Pablo Ave | Permanent supportive housing | January 2023 |
| Hope Center | 2012 Berkeley Way | Emergency shelter / PSH | September 2022 |

The question this analysis addresses is not whether shelters should exist. It is whether this concentration, on these corridors, produced measurable public safety costs that the city did not account for.

## Finding 1: The Shelter Corridors Diverged from the Rest of Berkeley

Using a trajectory index (2021 monthly average = 100), non-traffic police calls on Berkeley's primary commercial corridors evolved as follows through 2025:

| Corridor | 2021 | 2023 | 2025 | Trend |
|----------|------|------|------|-------|
| University Ave core | 98 | 112 | **129** | Worsening |
| San Pablo node | 103 | 128 | **171** | Worsening sharply |
| North Shattuck | 109 | 97 | **74** | Improving |
| South Telegraph | 106 | 117 | **100** | Flat to improving |
| Ashby Avenue | — | — | **84** | Improving |
| Adeline Street (south) | — | — | **80** | Improving |

Of 11 Berkeley corridors with sufficient call activity, University Avenue ranked 3rd worst and San Pablo ranked 5th — both above the midpoint while most of the city improved. South Telegraph, despite bearing the strain of the years-long People's Park crisis, returned to its 2021 baseline by 2025. The shelter corridors did not.

This divergence is the central finding of the analysis. It is not explained by citywide trends, which ran in the opposite direction.

---

## Finding 2: The Nature of Calls Changed, Not Just the Volume

Call volume increases alone could reflect many factors. What distinguishes the shelter corridors is the *type* of calls that increased — categories that directly degrade quality of life for residents and businesses.

**1461 University Ave** (opened July 2021):
- Theft calls: +101% (4.6 → 9.2 per month)
- Welfare checks: +91%
- Trespassing: +37%

**1619 University Ave** (opened July 2023, *partial occupancy*):
- Welfare checks: +173%
- Trespassing: +92%
- Noise disturbance: +307% (from a low base)

*Note: This 23-room facility (The Campus / Super 8) was originally intended for a specific encampment population that declined placement. The city pivoted to a different group. As of February 2024 only 14 of 23 rooms were occupied. Post-opening figures reflect partial occupancy and likely understate the impact at full capacity.*

**Golden Bear Inn / 1620 San Pablo** (opened January 2023):
- Disturbance calls: +305%
- Welfare checks: +704%
- Mental health calls: +816%
- Noise: +346%

These are not abstract statistics. Theft harms neighboring businesses and residents. Welfare checks consume officer time and signal visible distress on the street. The dramatic increases at the Golden Bear Inn — across every category simultaneously — indicate a facility generating sustained, broad-spectrum neighborhood impact.

**1761 University Ave** (opened February 2026) has only weeks of post-opening data, but person-down calls have already spiked 395% and battery calls 146%. This site warrants close monitoring.

---

## Finding 3: The Impact Radiates Beyond the Immediate Sites

The Hope Center at 2012 Berkeley Way sits at the eastern terminus of Berkeley Way, one block from Shattuck Avenue. In May–June 2022 — as the facility was completing construction and becoming a known destination — non-traffic calls on North Shattuck nearly doubled from a stable baseline of ~33/month to 77/month. That level has been sustained through the end of the study period, running 50–75 calls/month.

This represents a ~70% permanent step-change on a corridor that had shown no upward trend in the preceding 18 months of data. The timing is consistent with the Hope Center pre-opening period and inconsistent with any other identifiable local change.

The specific mechanism driving North Shattuck's increase has not been independently measured. What the data establishes is that a sustained elevation in public safety demand appeared on an adjacent corridor concurrent with the Hope Center's activation, and has not subsided. Whether that reflects activity by facility residents, service provider traffic, or secondary congregation patterns, the operational cost falls on North Shattuck businesses and residents who bear no direct relationship to the facility.

This cascade effect means the public safety cost of shelter siting is not contained within the 100-meter buffer of each facility.

## Finding 4: Stop Data Independently Corroborates the Call Findings

Police calls reflect citizen-reported demand. Stop data reflects officer-initiated action. The two datasets are independent; their convergence strengthens the finding.

**Pedestrian stops near shelter sites, pre- vs. post-opening:**

| Site | Pre-opening rate | Post-opening rate | Change |
|------|-----------------|------------------|--------|
| 1461 University Ave | 0.90/mo | 2.38/mo | +164% |
| 1619 University Ave | 0.50/mo | 0.56/mo | +12% |
| Golden Bear Inn | 0.50/mo | 1.43/mo | +186% |

**Offense profile near 1461 University Ave** is dominated by theft and robbery, which account for 42.4% of pedestrian stops — nearly three times the citywide rate of 13.9%. This is not a welfare-check or mental-health profile. It is a property crime profile, indicating that the area around 1461 has become a significant locus of theft activity.

## Finding 5: Berkeley Taxpayers Are Funding Response to a County-Placed Population

Under California's Project Homekey and related programs, Alameda County places individuals into Berkeley facilities from across the county. The city builds and operates the shelter; the county determines who occupies it. Berkeley taxpayers fund Berkeley Police Department response to whoever is placed there.

The stop data provides a direct test of this dynamic. Among pedestrian stops within 100 meters of 1461 University Ave, **20.8% involved individuals from outside Berkeley** — compared to a citywide baseline of 14.5% for all pedestrian stops. This represents a 43% relative elevation.

Alameda County does not disclose placement origin data by facility. The stop residence data is therefore the best available empirical evidence of the placement pipeline operating as described. It indicates that Berkeley's shelter capacity is drawing police response to a population that would, absent the siting decision, have been placed elsewhere or not placed at all.

A separate dataset corroborates the directional finding. A single month of Berkeley citywide arrest records (March–April 2026) shows that 24 of 190 arrests — 12.6% — involved individuals with outstanding warrants issued by other jurisdictions. Several of those individuals carried multiple simultaneous outside warrants. Analysis of a full year of arrest records previously identified a pattern of frequent repeat arrests of the same individuals, indicating these are not transient visitors passing through: they are residents of Berkeley who are wanted elsewhere and remain here because Berkeley's enforcement posture — catch-and-release at worst — offers a more stable operating environment than the jurisdictions seeking them. The non-local share of the arrest population (12.6% outside-warrant cases citywide) is directionally consistent with the non-local share of pedestrian stops near the shelter sites (20.8%), drawn from an independent dataset by an independent mechanism. Both signals point the same way.

## The Counterarguments and Why They Fall Short

**"These people were already in Berkeley and would have generated calls somewhere else."**
This is the displacement hypothesis. It assumes the placed population is drawn from Berkeley's existing unhoused residents. In practice, county placement decisions draw from a regional pool. Berkeley's willingness to build shelter capacity increases its allocation from that pool. Cities that do not build receive fewer placements. The resource flows are regional, not local.

**"Reduced policing and reporting after George Floyd explain the trends."**
The opposite is true: if defunding sentiment suppressed both proactive policing and citizen reporting in Berkeley — which it demonstrably did — then the observed increases near shelter sites occurred *against* that headwind. This makes the documented increases more significant, not less. Moreover, if underenforcement were driving citywide trends upward, every corridor would worsen together. The divergence between University Avenue and improving corridors like Telegraph and Ashby cannot be explained by a uniform citywide force.

**"The data only shows correlation, not causation."**
True of any observational study. But the evidence converges across five independent data sources (call volume, call type, corridor index, stop frequency, stop offense profile), tracks closely with opening dates, and runs counter to citywide trends. The pattern is consistent with causation and inconsistent with coincidence.

## Finding 6: The Full Fiscal Cost Has Not Been Tallied

Public safety demand is one component of the cost Berkeley incurs from these siting decisions. The full ledger is considerably larger and, to date, has not been publicly accounted for.

**Direct facility costs.** Berkeley leases the former Super 8 / The Campus (1619 University Ave) at $110 per room per night for 23 rooms, plus a $2,200 annual damage deposit per room. Per the executed lease approved by City Council on July 11, 2023, total costs are not to exceed $976,580 in year 1, escalating 3% annually: $1,005,877 (year 2), $1,036,054 (year 3), $1,067,136 (year 4), and $1,099,149 (year 5) — a five-year total of **$5,184,796**. Service provider contracts are on top of this figure. State Encampment Resolution Funding covers only the first two years (August 2023–July 2025); years 3–5 require additional funding sources not yet identified at time of approval.<sup>[A]</sup>

**Matching and co-funding obligations.** Homekey and related state programs require local matching funds. Berkeley has drawn on Measure P proceeds and general fund allocations to satisfy those matches. When state Encampment Resolution Funding for the 1619 University Ave lease expires in July 2025, the city has identified Measure P as the intended replacement source, per the same July 11, 2023 council agenda packet that authorized the lease.<sup>[A]</sup>

Measure P, approved by Berkeley voters in 2020, increases the real property transfer tax from 1.5% to 2.5% for ten years on sales and transfers over $1,500,000 — calibrated to capture approximately the top third of transactions by value. The proceeds are designated for navigation centers, mental health support, rehousing, and related homeless services.

The circularity is notable. Measure P's tax base consists of high-value commercial and residential transactions — exactly the category of redevelopment that could otherwise occur on corridors like University Avenue west of MLK. When the city converts a motel to nonprofit shelter instead of facilitating private redevelopment, it forfeits both the ongoing property tax revenue and any future Measure P transfer tax that a high-value sale would have generated. It then uses Measure P proceeds — drawn from other transactions — to cover the lease costs of the conversion. Meanwhile, the Measure P revenue pledged to shelter leases is unavailable for streets, parks, fire response, or other core services the measure nominally does not fund but that competing general-fund priorities require. Berkeley is servicing the ongoing cost of decisions that reduce the tax base with a tax stream whose yield depends on the health of the same market those decisions suppress.

**Litigation.** Activist organizations and legal-aid firms — some partially funded through city and county contracts — have used the Americans with Disabilities Act and related federal law to constrain Berkeley's options on encampment enforcement, shelter placement standards, and service requirements. Berkeley has expended city attorney resources and outside counsel defending and responding to these claims. The cost is not publicly itemized.

**The non-congregate mandate.** During the COVID-19 period, activist pressure successfully established a political norm that only non-congregate (single-occupancy room) shelter was acceptable. Berkeley has continued to operate under that constraint. Non-congregate shelter costs materially more per bed than traditional congregate models — the Super 8 lease rate reflects this premium. A city that had retained the political flexibility to use congregate shelter would be spending significantly less per placement.

**Property tax attrition.** When a privately owned motel transfers to a nonprofit operator, the parcel exits the property tax rolls. The revenue loss is permanent. Berkeley has converted multiple such properties without any public accounting of the aggregate assessed-value reduction or its long-run impact on the general fund that funds police, fire, and public works.

The combined effect is a city spending substantially on facility leases, service contracts, matching obligations, and litigation — while simultaneously incurring elevated public safety costs on affected corridors, and forgoing the property tax base that would fund those services. None of these cost streams have been presented to residents in an integrated accounting.

## What the City Did Not Do

Berkeley approved these siting decisions without:

- A systematic framework for measuring neighborhood public safety impact before or after opening
- Public disclosure of placement origin data from the county
- A process for monitoring outcomes and adjusting policy if impacts exceeded projections
- Any accounting for the incremental police and EMS costs imposed on the general fund
- Any accounting for the permanent property tax exemption created when motel parcels transferred to nonprofit ownership

In at least one documented case, a city council member voted to approve a siting decision and subsequently scheduled a community meeting — reversing the democratic sequence that would allow affected residents to inform the decision.

---

## Conclusions

The data supports a clear finding: concentrating interim and supportive housing facilities on Berkeley's western University Avenue corridor and San Pablo Avenue produced measurable, sustained increases in public safety demand that diverge sharply from citywide trends. The increases are not limited to call volume — they include a shift toward categories of calls and stops that directly harm neighboring residents and businesses.

The city incurred these costs without measuring them, without disclosing the assumptions behind siting decisions, and without creating any mechanism for accountability if outcomes were negative.

The purpose of this analysis is not to argue against housing for people experiencing homelessness. It is to establish that siting decisions have consequences, those consequences are measurable, and the residents who bear them deserve a city government that measures them before voting.
